Antioxidant

Proso millet, a cereal crop with potential health benefits, has not been extensively studied in terms of its nutritional value. To address this knowledge gap, Li et.al (2021) conducted a UHPLC-QqQ-MS/MS-based metabolomics analysis of whole grains from four proso millet varieties with different bran colors (White, Black, Gray, and Red) [1] .This study identified a total of 672 metabolites, including 121, 116, and 148 metabolites that showed differential accumulation in the three comparison groups (White vs. Black/Gray/Red). Results revealed that tryptophan metabolism and flavonoid, isoflavonoid, flavone, and flavonol biosynthesis were the main pathways that were differentially activated. Additionally, they observed significant differences in the accumulation of phenolic acids and flavonoids among the four varieties, which may contribute to variations in antioxidant activities. These findings provide useful insights for further exploration of the food chemistry of proso millet and for optimizing its utilization as a special crop.

In summary, this study sheds light on the metabolic basis of the nutritional value of proso millet, and highlights the potential health-promoting properties of this cereal crop. The identified pathways and metabolites can serve as a foundation for future research aimed at developing proso millet-based functional foods and nutraceuticals.

The study reports on the phytochemical content, antioxidant activity, and antiproliferative properties of three different varieties of proso millet. The phenolic content of the millet samples was measured in both the free and bound fractions, with the bound fraction contributing between 62.08% and 67.05% of the total phenolic content. Ferulic acid and chlorogenic acid were found to be the predominant phenolic acids in the bound fraction, while syringic acid was only detected in the free fraction. The antioxidant activity of the millet samples was measured using the hydrophilic peroxyl radical scavenging capacity assay. The PSC antioxidant activity of the free fraction ranged from 57.68 to 147.32 µmol of vitamin C equiv/100 g DW, while the PSC antioxidant activity of the bound fraction ranged from 95.38 to 136.48 µmol of vitamin C equiv/100 g DW. The cellular antioxidant activity of the extract was assessed using the HepG2 model, and values ranged from 2.51 to 6.10 µmol equiv quercetin/100 g DW. Finally, the antiproliferative activities of the proso millet samples were assessed in vitro against MDA human breast cancer and HepG2 human liver cancer cells. The results showed a differential and potentially selective antiproliferative property of the proso millet. Overall, the study suggests that proso millet has potential health benefits due to its antioxidant and antiproliferative properties. These results may be useful in directing the consumption of proso millet as part of a healthy diet [4] .
