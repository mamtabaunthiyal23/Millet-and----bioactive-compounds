Production

In 2021, the production of proso millet in the United States reached 16.8 million bushels, a significant increase from the previous year's production of 9.21 million bushels. This increase was due to both an expansion in acreage and higher yields. The crop was valued at $150 million, with an average price of $8.92 per bushel. The largest producers of proso millet were Colorado, Nebraska, and South Dakota. Proso millet is a versatile crop that can be grown on a variety of soil types and is well-suited for poor-quality land with low water holding capacity and fertility. It is often used as an emergency cash crop, planted when other crops fail or are not able to be planted due to unfavorable conditions. Additionally, proso millet can be beneficial in a crop rotation, as it can enhance weed control, especially with winter annual grasses in winter wheat. The recommended planting rate for proso millet is 20-30 lbs of pure live seed per acre, and the crop is typically drill planted. However, growers should be aware that proso millet seeds do not mature uniformly, and shattering of early ripening seeds can be a common problem. To mitigate this issue, swathing before threshing is recommended, with growers beginning swathing when seeds in the upper half of the panicle have matured. Proso millet grain should be stored at 13 percent moisture or less, and if harvested at higher moisture content, it can be artificially dried. When feeding to livestock, the hard seed coat should be cracked to improve digestion. Proso millet is a good source of nutrition for birds and can be used for bird seed. Some proso millet undergoes a dehulling process for use in human and animal food products. For swine and poultry, proso millet should be supplemented with lysine and checked for adequacy of calcium and B vitamins.

Value added products :-

    1. Flour production:-

    To produce couscous, porridge, and biscuits, refined forms of the proso millet grains were used. The proso millet was first refined and milled by Satake-USA in Stafford, TX using the procedure described in Anderson (2014). Corn meal was also milled to produce flour by Bunge in St. Louis, MO. Both the proso millet flour and grains were shipped to the University of Guelph in Guelph, ON for storage in an 18 C freezer until product testing.

2.Biscuit preparation:-

To make each biscuit, a mixture of 248 g of proso millet flour, baking powder, egg, unsalted butter, salt, and milk was prepared. The batter was thoroughly mixed, and 47 g of it was placed into a 7.6 cm diameter muffin mold before being baked at 425 F for 15 minutes. Once the biscuits were manufactured, they were stored in an 18 C freezer for up to 2 months. The night before the trial, the biscuits were removed from the freezer to thaw.

3. Alcoholic beverages

Tongba is a fermented millet beverage that is popular in the far eastern mountainous region of Nepal and Sikkim, India. Millet is used as the base ingredient for various alcoholic beverages in India, including the distilled liquor rakshi. The name "Tongba" actually refers to the vessel used to hold the fermented millet beverage known as "mandokpenaa thee." To prepare mandokpenaa thee, whole grain millet is cooked and fermented. After the millet is cooked and cooled, it is mixed with khesung, which is a source of molds, bacteria, and yeast. The mixture is then collected and placed in a woven bamboo basket lined with green leaves or plastic. The basket is covered with thick folds of cloth and left in a warm place for 1-2 days, depending on the temperature. Once the fermentation is complete, the sweet mass is packed tightly into an earthenware pot or plastic jar. The opening of the pot is usually sealed off to prevent air from entering. After 7-15 days, depending on the temperature, the fermentation process is complete and the mass is converted into mandokpenaa thee. The longer mandokpenaa thee is left undisturbed in the pot, the more it matures, intensifying its flavors while also becoming more mellow. It is traditionally stored for about six months.

4. Awaokoshi

Awaokoshi, a traditional Japanese sweet, is a specialty of Osaka, Japan. The tradition of making this millet confection began over 1000 years ago during the early Heian period, when it was presented to Sugawara no Michizane during a stop in Naniwa. Awaokoshi is a type of cereal puff that is candied and compressed into bars, and it is considered one of Japan's oldest known sweets. Amidaike Daikoku, a long-standing establishment in Osaka, has been known for producing high-quality awaokoshi that appeals to discerning Osakan taste buds.
