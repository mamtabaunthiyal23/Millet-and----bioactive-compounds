Cultivation

Preparation of land

Pearl millet is a versatile crop that can be cultivated in various types of soil, although it does not thrive in waterlogged conditions. To ensure optimal growth, it is recommended to plow the field once or twice and then follow up with harrowing to create a finely-tilled soil surface. This will help to prepare the soil bed for planting and promote healthy root development, leading to a better crop yield.

Method of sowing

Pearl millet can be sown using three different methods: (1) on a flat surface, (2) using the ridge and furrow system, or (3) on a broad-bed and furrow system. It is recommended that the seed be sown at a depth of 2.5 cm to 3 cm for optimal growth.

Time of sowing

For optimal growth, it is recommended to sow kharif pearl millet during the first fortnight of July in the northern and central regions of the country. In Tamil Nadu, the appropriate time for rabi season sowing is during the first fortnight of October. If the population is scanty, gap filling can be done by transplanting seedlings 2-3 weeks after sowing. In the Marathwada area of Maharashtra, dry sowing before the first monsoon rains is advised. For higher summer pearl millet production in zone B, sowing should be done between the 4th and 5th Standard Meteorological Week, which falls between the last week of January and the first week of February.

Seed treatment

To protect against soilborne diseases, seed treatment with either Trichoderma harzianum at a rate of 4g per kg of seed or thiram 75% dust at a rate of 3g per kg of seed is recommended. For the control of smut disease, the seeds should be treated with 300-mesh sulfur powder at a rate of 4g per kg of seeds. Ergot affected seeds can be treated by soaking them in a 10% salt solution. Downy mildew can be controlled by seed treatment with metalaxyl (Apron 35 SD) at a rate of 6g per kg of seed. Additionally, the availability of nitrogen and phosphorus can be enhanced by treating the seeds with Azospirillum (at a rate of 600g) and Phosphobacterium.

Seed rate

3 kg/ha

Spacing

In the arid-western plain regions of Rajasthan, Haryana, and Kutch of Gujarat (A1 zone), pearl millet should be planted in rows 60 cm apart with a low plant population of 1.00 to 1.25 lakh per hectare. However, for areas that receive rainfall of more than 450 mm (zone A & B), the crop should be planted with a spacing of 45 x 10-15 cm and a higher plant population of 1.75 to 2.0 lakhs per hectare. To achieve the required plant stand, a seed rate of 3 to 4 kg per hectare is recommended.

Plant population

Under normal conditions, the recommended plant stand for pearl millet is 180,000 plants per hectare or 72,000 plants per acre. However, under irrigation or when managing highly productive soils, a higher population of 225,000 plants per hectare (100,000 plants per acre) is recommended. On extremely sandy and drought-prone soils, a lower population of about 90,000 plants per hectare (40,000 plants per acre) is desirable.

Inter cultivation and weed control

A comparison of different weed control methods showed that two rounds of hoeing and weeding at 15 and 30 days after sowing (DAS) effectively controlled weeds. This method was found to be comparable in effectiveness to pre-emergent application of atrazine at a rate of 0.5 kg/ha, followed by one hand weeding. Additionally, the second weeding was found to have the added benefit of conserving soil moisture.

Pearl millet-based cropping systems in kharif

To avoid problems with downy mildew disease, it is recommended to practice crop rotation by using different cultivars. This can be achieved by alternating the use of pearl millet hybrids and open-pollinated varieties each year or season. It is important to avoid planting the same hybrid or open-pollinated variety on the same plot of land continuously, as this can lead to increased susceptibility to downy mildew and other diseases. By adopting a rotation strategy, growers can help reduce the risk of disease and maintain the health of their crops.

Haryana Pearl millet + Green gram
Uttar Pradesh Pearl millet + Green gram
Madhya Pradesh Pearl millet + Black gram
Punjab Pearl millet + Chickpea
Delhi Pearl millet + Pigeonpea

Irrigation

To improve crop yield during extended periods of drought, it is essential to apply irrigation during critical stages of crop growth such as tillering, flowering, and grain development, provided that there is access to water. For example, in the summertime, it is recommended to irrigate pearl millet at regular intervals using a recommended rate of 0.75-1.0IW/CPE and 40mm of water, depending on the specific needs of the crop. This practice will help ensure optimal growth and yield for the crop

Harvesting

For optimum yield and quality, pearl millet should be harvested at the stage of physiological maturity, which can be determined by the black spot found at the bottom of the grain in the hilar region. At this stage, the leaves of the plant will have turned yellowish and the grains will be hard and firm. It is a common practice to first cut the earheads and then the stalks after a week, once they have sufficiently dried. The stalks (straw) are then collected, allowed to dry, and stacked for later use. It is recommended to ensure that the grain has a moisture content of 14% or less at the time of harvesting. For long-term storage, which is typically longer than 6 months, the grain should have a moisture content of less than 12% to prevent spoilage. By following these guidelines, farmers can ensure that their pearl millet crop will be of the highest quality and can be stored effectively for future use.

Pests

  • Grey weevil
  • Stem borers
  • Hairy caterpillars
  • White Grub

Diseases

  • Smut
  • Ergot
  • Blast
  • Downy mildew
