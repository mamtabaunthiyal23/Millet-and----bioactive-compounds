Antioxidant Activity

Pushparaj and Urooj (2014) conducted a study to investigate the impact of different processing methods, including milling, boiling, pressure cooking, roasting, and germination, on the antioxidant activity and components of pearl millet, specifically the Kalukombu (K) and Maharashtra Rabi Bajra (MRB) cultivars. The study utilized several common assays for measuring antioxidant activity, such as the Reducing Power Assay (RPA), Ferric Reducing Antioxidant Power (FRAP), and 1,1-Diphenyl-2-Picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) free radical scavenging activity. Results showed that the antioxidant activity of pearl millet was influenced by both the processing methods and the cultivars. The K cultivar exhibited a higher content of antioxidant components, which reflected its higher antioxidant capacity compared to MRB. The Bran Rich Fraction was found to have high antioxidant activity in terms of RPA, which was attributed to its high levels of tannins, phytic acid, and flavonoids. The millet subjected to various heat treatments exhibited higher antioxidant activity, particularly DPPH scavenging activity and RPA, mainly due to its flavonoid content. Interestingly, an inverse relationship was found between radical scavenging activity and yield. The heat-treated millet with significantly (P ≤ 0.05) high radical scavenging activity had the lowest yield, while the germinated millet, which showed the lowest activity, had the highest yield. Overall, the study highlights the importance of processing methods and cultivar selection in enhancing the antioxidant activity and components of pearl millet [1].

In 2013, Odusola et al. conducted a study to determine the nutritional composition and antioxidant properties of pearl millet [5] . The methanol extract of the millet sample was evaluated for its antioxidant potential, which was determined using several methods, including reducing property, total phenolic content, and scavenging activity on 2,2-Diphenyl-1-Picrylhydrazyl (DPPH). The results of the study showed that pearl millet contains high levels of antioxidants, particularly phenolic compounds, which have been linked to potential anticancer properties. The millet also exhibited high phenolic content, moderate reducing ability, and high free radical scavenging activity, which suggests its potential health benefits. Studies by Sharma and Kapoor (1996) have reported that pearl millet grains contain 608.1mg/100g of phenols, while pearl millet flour contains 761mg/100g. These phenolic compounds, especially flavonoids, have been found to inhibit tumor development and are concentrated in the pericarp and testa of the grain. As traditionally, the entire pearl millet grain is milled, products made from the flour would provide the health benefits of the flavonoids and phenols. Overall, the study suggests that pearl millet may be a beneficial addition to the diet due to its high antioxidant properties and potential anticancer properties.
