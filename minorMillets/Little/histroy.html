History

Little millet, also known as Indian millet, originated in the Indian peninsula and is primarily cultivated in India and Sri Lanka, as well as some surrounding countries. It is a self-pollinated crop with low cross-pollination rates. The species is divided into two races, nana and robusta, with nana plants varying in height from 60 to 170 cm and robusta plants growing between 120 to 190 cm tall. Little millet is adapted to a wide range of climates, from temperate to tropical, and can tolerate both drought and waterlogging. The crop is mainly grown in hilly areas in India and covers about 500,000 hectares of land. Little millet is an important catch crop in some tribal farms in India. While the antiquity of P. sumatrense cultivation is unknown, little millet is thought to have an ancient domestication in India due to its wide distribution as a small millet across most agricultural zones. The species is similar in habit to proso millet but smaller, growing as an annual herbaceous plant with linear leaves and panicles ranging from 4 to 15 cm in length. The grain of little millet is round and smooth, measuring between 1.8 to 1.9 mm in length. Despite its name, little millet is not native to Sumatra, although the species name is based on a specimen collected from there.
