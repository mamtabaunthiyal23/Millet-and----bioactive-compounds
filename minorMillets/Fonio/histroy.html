History

Fonio, also known as po to Mali's Dogon people, is considered to be the oldest cereal in West Africa and plays a significant role in their cosmogony. It is believed to be the "germ of the world" and represents agriculture. According to Roland Porteres, a famous French agronomist, fonio is grown between latitudes 8 and 14 North, from Senegal to Lake Chad, and is supplanted by finger millet further east. White fonio, or Digitaria exilis, is primarily grown in Guinea and is a staple for people in the mountain regions of Fouta Djalon, as well as in Mali, Burkina Faso, Ivory Coast, Nigeria, Senegal, and Benin. Fonio has been used as a food source for centuries, with the first references dating back to the mid-14th century by Berber explorer Ibn Battûta. The grain is given various names depending on the region and ethnicity, but they all have the same meaning of "food" or "something to eat". Fonio is a small annual herbaceous plant that produces tiny grains surrounded by a husk, and it is cultivated in a variety of environmental conditions, from tropical monsoons to hot, arid deserts. Extra-early maturing fonio varieties can produce mature grains in as little as 70-90 days, making it one of the fastest maturing cereals. It is often grown to avoid food shortages during the lean season, earning it the nickname "hungry rice". Fonio is also drought-tolerant and well-suited for nutrient-poor, sandy soils, though it does show some unfavorable characteristics such as residual seed shattering, lodging, and lower yields than other cereals. The most likely wild progenitor of cultivated fonio is the tetraploid annual weed D. longiflora, which is widely distributed in tropical Africa. Fonio was likely domesticated over 5000 years ago in the Inner Niger Delta of central Mali, with some selection for reduced seed shattering and spikelet hairiness occurring in the process. Recently, fonio has gained popularity both inside and outside of West Africa due to its nutritional qualities.
