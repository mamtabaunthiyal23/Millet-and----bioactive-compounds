Antioxidant

A study conducted by Prashant S. Hegde and T.S. Chandra investigated six different millets, namely kodo millet (Paspalum scrobiculatum), finger millet (Eleusine coracana), little millet (Panicum miliare), foxtail millet (Setaria italica), barnyard millet (Echinochloa utilis), and great millet (Sorghum bicolor) that are grown in India. The white varieties of these millets were evaluated for their ability to scavenge 1,1-Diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) radicals using electron spin resonance (ESR). The results showed that the methanol extracts of kodo millet flour exhibited the highest DPPH quenching activity (70%) compared to other millet extracts (15-53%). The white varieties of great millet, finger millet, and foxtail millet showed lower quenching activity than their colored counterparts, indicating that the phenolics present in the seed coat may be responsible for their antioxidant activities. However, the content of phenols and tannins in these grains did not correlate with their antioxidant activities. Kodo millet had the highest DPPH quenching activity, followed by great millet and finger millet. The antioxidant activity of kodo and finger millet was reduced when cooked by roasting or boiling. Furthermore, fractionation of kodo millet into husk and endosperm also decreased the antioxidant activity, suggesting that the phytochemicals in the grain may act synergistically. [1]
