Production

Kodo millet is a popular crop grown in several regions of India, including Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Tamilnadu, West Bengal, Madhya Pradesh, and Andhra Pradesh. In 2015-16, the crop was cultivated across a total area of 1.96 lakh hectares in the country, resulting in a total production of approximately 0.84 lakh tonnes. This translates to a productivity rate of 429 kg/ha, indicating that kodo millet is a relatively high-yielding crop in India.

Value Added products Using Kodo millet

Millet is a staple food in many parts of the world, and its grains are considered suitable for processing at both domestic and industrial levels. With growing awareness of its prospective health advantages and disease-fighting properties, millet is attracting increasing interest from food technologists and nutritionists. However, in many developed countries, millets remain underutilized, while in developing nations, there is enormous potential for processing millet grains into value-added foods and drinks. As a result, countries around the world are investing in research to study and develop advanced processing technologies at the industrial level, with the aim of improving the nutritive value and functionality of millet products and enhancing customer satisfaction.

  • Blended flour :-

    • Millets are known to be more nutritious than many cereals, yet their utilization in food products remains limited. One way to increase their consumption is by blending millet flour with wheat flour after suitable processing. However, the addition of millet flour to other products can alter the physico-chemical, nutritive, and functional characteristics of the blended product. Many convenience food products, including extruded products, are consumed widely in developed countries. Extruded products prepared using millets have been found to have higher nutritive value compared to weaning foods of acceptable quality. Researchers have explored the use of composite millet flours, substituting commonly used cereal flours, to manufacture various food products, such as ready-to-use foods and pasta. This approach is a convenient way to create nutritionally improved products with increased functionality.

  • Baked products :-

The popularity of bakery products continues to increase worldwide due to their taste variation, low cost, varying texture profiles, long shelf life, and attractive packaging options. The use of millets in bakery products could not only increase the fiber content and micronutrient value but also create potential for millets to enter the bakery world as a series of value-added products. Most bakery products are traditionally prepared using wheat flour, but efforts are being made to replace a portion of it with millets to provide an alternative and reduce over-dependence on wheat, as well as to create gluten-free bread options

  • Fermented products

Fermented foods, such as Dosa and Idli, are widely consumed in South India and are popular globally as a significant source of nutrition. Cereal-based fermented foods are particularly prevalent, and millet-based fermented products are gaining popularity due to their potential health benefits. Fermentation of ground germinated pearl millet grains induces chemical changes that significantly increase protein digestibility, up to 90%. Scientific research suggests that producing millet-based fermented products, including Kodo millet, using fermentation and enzymatic hydrolysis processes can be a lucrative approach to enhancing their nutritive value and reducing anti-nutritive factors. Therefore, incorporating fermented millet-based products into the diet could have potential health benefits and could be a profitable business opportunity.

  • Puffed/popped and flaked millets :-

The practice of cooking grains to make puffed products for use as snacks or breakfast cereals is an old tradition. Cereal processing technologies have been effectively applied to foxtail millet, resulting in ready-to-eat (RTE) products such as flaked, extruded, roller-dried, decorticated, and popped grains. This is achieved by subjecting native grains with a moisture content of 12% to a high-temperature short-time (HTST) treatment at 230+/- 5 oC. These products can be consumed plain or with added spices, salt, or sweeteners.
