Production

Barnyard millet, belonging to the Echinochloa species, has a long history of cultivation in warm and temperate regions across the world, particularly in Asia, where it is widely grown in countries such as India, China, Japan, and Korea. It is considered one of the most important minor millets and plays a vital role in providing food security to many impoverished communities globally. In recent years, India has emerged as the largest producer of barnyard millet, with an average productivity of 1034 kg/ha during the last three years, producing 0.147 million tonnes on 0.146 million hectares of land. Barnyard millet is primarily cultivated for human consumption, but it is also used as livestock feed. It is a short-duration crop that can grow in adverse environmental conditions with minimal inputs and can withstand various biotic and abiotic stresses, making it an ideal crop for small farmers who lack resources and infrastructure. Among the many cultivated and wild species of barnyard millet, Echinochloa frumentacea (Indian barnyard millet) and Echinochloa esculenta (Japanese barnyard millet) are two of the most popular. With its hardy nature and versatility, barnyard millet has the potential to contribute significantly to sustainable agriculture and food security in many parts of the world.

Value added products

  • Vermicelli

Vermicelli, macaroni, and pasta have become a popular choice among children and adults alike due to their delicious taste, affordability, and ease of preparation. However, pasta is not just a tasty treat, but it can also serve as a foundation for healthy and nutritious meals when paired with nutrient-dense food partners like fiber-rich vegetables and beans, heart-healthy fish and oils, antioxidant-packed tomato sauce, and protein-rich cheeses, eggs, poultry, and lean meats. To compete with other important cereal foods, small millets need to be incorporated into preprocessed or alternative millet-based foods. With this in mind, vermicelli, noodles, and macaroni have been produced using refined wheat flour blended with small millets at a 30% incorporation level. By doing so, we can provide consumers with healthier options while also promoting the consumption of small millets.

  • Fermented food

Dosa and Idli, two popular fermented foods in India, are commonly eaten for breakfast and dinner in the southern part of the country. Little millet, a nutritious grain rich in protein, calcium, and fiber, is a key ingredient in these dishes, enhancing their flavor and nutritional value. Sprouted barnyard millet or malted grains are also used in the fermentation process, depending on personal preference. Overall, these fermented foods are an excellent way to incorporate millets into one's diet and enjoy their health benefits.

  • Bakery products

Bakery products are beloved by people of all ages due to their delicious taste, appealing colors, and easy digestibility. These baked goods have become an integral part of celebrating happy moments. In today's fast-paced world, individuals have limited time to spend on making breakfast, so bread, buns, biscuits, and other baked goods have become a popular choice. They serve as a convenient snack option and are widely available. To improve the nutritional value of bakery products, small millets have been incorporated in various proportions ranging from 10% to 50% to replace refined wheat flour. For instance, bread is standardized with 20% small millet, cake with 30%, cookies with 50%, soup sticks with 20%, and khari with 40%. These variations not only enhance the nutritional value of the baked goods but also add a unique flavor and texture to them.

  • Flaked products

Cereal popped products and flakes are a popular breakfast option, and currently, they are mostly made from corn. However, by utilizing suitable processing techniques, it is feasible to produce popped foods and flakes from millets as well. These Ready-To-Eat products have gained immense popularity due to their crisp and friable texture. The relatively smaller size and quick hydration properties of millets make them a suitable option for the production of flakes and popped products. To improve the variety of breakfast options, small millets were flaked and popped, and several recipes were standardized. These millet-based products offer a nutritious and tasty alternative to corn-based breakfast cereals.
