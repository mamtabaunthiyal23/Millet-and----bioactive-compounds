History

Barnyard millet is an ancient crop cultivated in warm and temperate regions around the world, particularly in the semi-arid tropics of Asia and Africa, including countries like India, China, Japan, and Korea. In India, this millet is grown from the Himalayan region in the north to the Deccan plateau in the south, from hilly areas to marginal areas. The Echinochloa genus comprises 35 species found in warmer and temperate regions, and Barnyard millet is the common name given to most of these species, all of which are native to Southern or Eastern Asia. The Indian-based millet is believed to have originated from undomesticated species of Echinochloa, such as Echinochloa Colona (jungle rice), and exhibits a simultaneous evolution in India and Africa. Despite being an ancient crop, there is no evidence to date regarding the genetic source or origin of this crop, and this lack of clarity hinders the way of having better understanding of this species through morphological perspective, because there are almost negligible mutants phenotypically. In Japan, rice was regarded as a staple food in the Japanese diet, but the agronomic and climatic conditions did not support rice farming. Therefore, barnyard millet (Japanese-based), which is capable of surviving in harsh conditions, eventually became the staple food for the Japanese. In the 1920s, barnyard millet production and consumption peaked, and rice was considered a luxurious food item. People there preferred millet over rice for daily consumption or mixed equal portions of both millet and rice, and this continued until the 1940s. However, with innovations and growing technology, tools and techniques were developed to benefit rice cultivation in Japan, leading to a gradual decline in the production of barnyard millet. Statistics from their study reveal a decrease in barnyard millet production over time. Japanese barnyard millet production decreased from 103,600 ha in 1880 to 5090 ha in 1969 and 285 ha in 1984. Barnyard millet is amongst the top 4 most produced millets globally, and India is the top producer of barnyard millet, not only in terms of area (0.146 m/ha) but also in cultivation (0.147 MT) with an average productivity (1034 kg/ha) over the last 3 years . Primarily, barnyard millet is grown for human consumption, but at times it is also used as fodder. In India, barnyard millet is the second most important minor millet following finger millet, with a production and productivity of 87 thousand tons and 875 kg/ha.
