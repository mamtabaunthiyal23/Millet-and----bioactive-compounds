Production

Ethiopia is the world's largest producer of Teff, with 24% of the grain area dedicated to Teff in 2017, followed by maize at 17% and sorghum at 15%. The two major regions for Teff production are Amhara and Oromia, which collectively account for 85.5% of the Teff area and 87.8% of the Teff production. Teff is a low-risk crop, which has made it popular among smallholder farmers in Ethiopia. In fact, Teff research programs were initiated in Ethiopia as early as the late 1950s, with a focus on breeding improved Teff varieties to enhance production. Over the years, changes in the types of cultivated Teff have been observed, with an increase in white-colored Teff at the expense of red and mixed-colored Teff. The color of Teff grains, white, mixed, and red, mostly determines its grade for quality and price. White Teff commands the highest price due to its mild flavor and rigorous growing conditions, while red Teff commands the lowest price. Despite efforts to increase adoption rates of improved Teff seeds, limited access and unaffordable prices remain a challenge for smallholder farmers.

Value added products

  • Chapati :-

    • Teff flour is a nutritious alternative to traditional wheat or all-purpose flour. It is high in protein, which can promote feelings of fullness and reduce cravings. Additionally, its high fiber content can help manage blood sugar levels, decrease appetite, and aid in weight loss. Teff flour can be substituted for up to 50% of wheat or all-purpose flour in recipes. When making dough with teff flour, it is important to knead the dough and gradually add water until the desired consistency is achieved. Using teff flour in chapatti recipes may result in a slightly different color, but it should not affect the texture or flavor of the finished product. Overall, incorporating teff flour into recipes can be a healthy and flavorful choice.

  • Porridge

    • Preparing a delicious and nutritious Teff breakfast porridge is a breeze, and it only takes about 15 minutes. To get started, let's take a look at the ingredients required. To make the porridge base, you'll need some fonio, water, a plant-based milk of your choice, and a sweetener. While I used almond milk in my recipe, feel free to experiment with any other type of plant-based milk that you prefer. I found that the nutty flavour of fonio paired exceptionally well with almond milk. As for sweeteners, erythritol was my choice, but you can use any sweetener you like, such as sugar, maple syrup, or honey.

  • Injera

    • Injera is a popular Ethiopian bread that is made using teff, a grain that shares similar nutritional properties with millet. Teff is readily available on Amazon. Ethiopian cuisine has many similarities with Indian cuisine, and the bread is prepared using sourdough that is fermented. The bread is cooked in a large pan and has a soft, fluffy texture that is similar to our Aappam. I will be sharing recipes for lentils and veggies that are commonly served with Injeras separately.
