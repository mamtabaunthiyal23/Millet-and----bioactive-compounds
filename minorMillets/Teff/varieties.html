Varieties

Teff is a diverse crop with several varieties, each with unique characteristics suited to specific growing conditions. While it is beyond the scope of this paper to discuss all the different types of teff in detail, there are generally three main categories: white, mixed (red, brown, white), and red/brown. Each type of teff has its own set of properties, such as taste, color, and nutritional content. Farmers may choose to grow a particular variety based on factors such as climate, soil type, and intended use. Despite these differences, all types of teff are highly valued for their nutritional benefits, resilience, and versatility in cooking.

  • White teff

    • White teff is the most highly valued and preferred variety of teff, but it can only be grown in specific regions of Ethiopia, such as the highlands. This type of teff requires the most rigorous growing conditions and is, therefore, the most expensive. Similar to how white bread has been considered a status symbol in the United States, white teff was historically reserved for the wealthiest and most prestigious families in Ethiopia. The prestige associated with consuming white teff, as well as its more stringent growing conditions, contribute to its higher cost. Injera, a popular Ethiopian flatbread, is traditionally made with teff flour, and the use of white teff in particular can extend the shelf life of the bread. Despite its high cost, white teff remains a beloved and culturally significant crop in Ethiopia.

  • Red/brown teff

    • Red/brown teff, which is the least expensive and least preferred type, actually has the highest iron content of all teff varieties. Studies have found that individuals living in regions where red teff consumption is prevalent tend to have higher hemoglobin levels and a lower risk of anemia related to parasitic infection. As more research highlights the health benefits associated with high iron content in red teff, it is gaining more acceptance in Ethiopian society. While the available data composition tables do not differentiate between the iron content of red and white teff, the average iron content of teff grain is 62.71mg/4oz. It is currently unclear whether the iron content of the soil has an effect on the iron content of the grain, as there have been no studies investigating this relationship. However, it is possible that the high iron content of the soil may contribute to the iron content of the grain. Despite its lower status in Ethiopian society, the high iron content of red/brown teff makes it a valuable and nutritious crop.

  • Mixed teff

    • The third main type of teff is mixed, which contains a combination of red/brown and white varieties. This type of teff is generally considered to have moderate iron content. Mixed teff is more widely available than white teff and less expensive than both white and red/brown varieties. Mixed teff is often used to make injera, a staple food in Ethiopia. Injera made from mixed teff has a slightly different flavor and color than injera made from white teff.
