Cultivation

Temperature requirement

The temperature requirement of Teff is as follows;

To achieve optimal growth and weed control when planting teff, it is important to wait until soil temperatures have reached around 65 °F before planting. As a C-4 grass, teff thrives in hot temperatures and should be planted after other forages and row crops. Planting too early can lead to a slower establishment of the stand and give weeds an unnecessary advantage. To maximize teff growth, it is important to plant during periods of maximum heat unit accumulation. However, as the days become shorter in the fall, the growth rate of teff will slow down, and it will eventually stop growing after the first killing frost.

Soil types

Teff has been found to perform admirably across a wide range of soil types, including low-desert sands as well as waterlogged clays. Additionally, the species has shown to have a high tolerance for soil pH variability, thriving in both acidic and alkaline soils. While teff does react to salinity in a similar way to alfalfa, it has been found to perform even better than most sorghum types. Therefore, the selection of high-quality soil is essential in achieving optimal crop yields when farming teff for grain.

Crop Culture

Planting teff in late May, around the same time as millets, can be beneficial. Late plantings allow for tillage to control emerged weeds before planting, which can be particularly useful as teff is not a strong competitor with weeds during its early growth stages. Additionally, teff germinates quickly and can be effectively seeded using both broadcast and narrow row methods, allowing for stronger weed competition. To prevent lodging, it is recommended to apply moderate rates of nitrogen and phosphorus fertilizer. However, when harvesting teff for grain, several special considerations should be taken into account.

Fertility requirement

It is recommended to apply 25-60 kg/ha of nitrogen and 10-18 kg/ha of phosphorus for teff fertilization. Teff is known to respond more to nitrogen than to phosphorus, with higher nitrogen inputs resulting in increased biomass production and larger plants. However, excessive nitrogen inputs can also lead to lodging. To prevent lodging, farmers can decrease nitrogen input, opt to cultivate teff after a legume crop or adjust sowing time so that the crop reaches the heading stage after the rains have stopped. In Ethiopia, teff is commonly used in crop rotations alongside other cereals and legumes.

Seed Rate

The recommended seed rates for teff range from 4.5 to 9.0 kg/ha, and can be achieved using implements such as cult packers or brillion seeders. It is important to plant teff seeds at a depth of 12-15 mm, either through broadcasting or in narrow rows. For coated seeds, the recommended seeding rate is typically 5 to 7 pounds per acre. Seeding rates exceeding these ranges may result in lodging, loss of stand, and reduced crop yield. Therefore, it is important to exercise caution when selecting seed rates to achieve optimal crop growth and yield.

Harvesting

Teff is typically harvested 2 to 6 months after sowing, once the vegetative parts of the plant start to turn yellow. Harvesting teff after maturation can cause the seeds to fall off, particularly in windy or rainy conditions. In Ethiopia, the harvest season lasts from November to January, and is traditionally done manually using sickles. After cutting the plants at the soil surface, farmers pile them up in the field and transport them to a threshing area. Teff is typically threshed using animals walking on the harvest, although some farmers opt to rent threshing machines used for other cereals. The seeds are easy to store and can remain viable for several years if stored away from direct contact with humidity and sunlight. Average teff yields in Ethiopia reach around two tonnes per hectare, with one single inflorescence producing up to 1000 seeds and one plant up to 10,000. Teff also presents promising opportunities for breeding programs, with the first draft of the Eragrostis tef genome published in 2014 and ongoing research on selecting for more resistant varieties. In 1996, the US National Research Council highlighted teff as having the potential to improve nutrition, boost food security, foster rural development, and support sustainable land management.

Pests

Teff is susceptible to a range of insect pests, including the tef shoot fly, central shoot fly, Wello-bush cricket, red tef worm, tef epilachna beetle, chrysomelid black beetle Erlangerius niger, and stem-boring wasp Eurytomocharis eragrostidis. Proper pest management is critical to minimizing damage and ensuring optimal yield.
