Antioxidants

Teff millet is gaining worldwide recognition for its gluten-free nature and exceptional nutritional profile, making it a desirable nutraceutical food. This study analyzed the nutritional, phytochemical, and antioxidant properties of teff millet. The proximate analysis revealed that teff millet is a rich source of moisture, ash, protein, carbohydrate, and fiber, while being low in fat. The study also revealed the presence of essential minerals such as calcium, iron, phosphorus, and zinc. Furthermore, phytochemical analysis revealed the presence of flavonoids, saponins, tannin, glycosides, and steroids, contributing to its medicinal properties. Teff millet's high antioxidant activity was demonstrated by its ability to scavenge DPPH radicals. As such, incorporating teff millet into food products could have a positive impact on those with celiac disease, hypertension, anemia, diabetes, and cancer. The development of nutraceutical food products using teff millet has the potential to improve health outcomes and prevent various diseases and disorders. In conclusion, the nutritional and medicinal properties of teff millet make it an attractive ingredient in the food industry [1] .

In this study, researchers evaluated the total flavonoid and phenolic content, HPLC profile of the most common phenolics, and antioxidant activity of white and brown Eragrostis tef. They also examined the in vitro digestibility of the teff samples. The results showed that the content of flavonoids and phenolics, as well as antioxidant activity, were higher in the free phenolic fraction. The correlation analysis suggested that bound flavonoids did not significantly contribute to antioxidant activity. The researchers identified the main free phenolics in brown teff as trans-p-coumaric, protocatechuic, ferulic, and gallic acids, while the major free phenolics in white teff were rutin, protocatechuic, and ferulic acids. The main bound phenolics in brown teff were ferulic and gallic acids, quercetin, and catechin, while in white teff, they were ferulic acid, rutin, catechin, and quercetin. The in vitro digestibility of cooked teff was found to be very high, with a range of 80.5% to 85.1% organic matter digestibility. Interestingly, brown teff was significantly more digestible than white teff (P < 0.05). Overall, this study provides valuable insights into the composition and properties of teff and highlights the potential health benefits of its free phenolic fraction [2] .
